It Does Only One Thing

A language model never states a plan. Its one visible act, repeated: given the words so far, how probable is each possible next word? Whatever it intends must travel inside that single bet.

Everything a chatbot appears to do — reason, translate, joke, console — is delivered through that one operation, repeated. Below is the operation itself, running on your own device, with nothing hidden. Read the opening line, and the machine will publish its odds on what word should come next. It has no favourite until you give it one.

That favouritism is set by a single dial, called temperature. Turn it down and the machine becomes a pedant, always choosing the safest word. Turn it up and it turns feverish, giving long-shot words a real chance. Nothing else changes — the same odds, read with more or less nerve. Watch the columns below rise and fall as you turn it.

What a token is

The machine does not think in words but in tokens — word-fragments from a fixed vocabulary. Common words are one token; rarer ones are stitched from several. The odds above are cast over that whole vocabulary; only the leaders are shown. A leading space is part of the token, which is why some entries begin with a gap.

From logits to odds

Beneath the percentages sit logits — one raw score per entry in the vocabulary, emitted by the model's final layer. A logit has no scale of its own; it means nothing except against its neighbours. The softmax rule turns the whole set into proper odds: raise each score to an exponent, divide by the total. Every percentage on this sheet is a logit read through that rule.

What temperature really does

The logits never change. Temperature only stretches or squeezes them before they are read as odds. Near zero, the top score swamps all others and the machine is nearly deterministic. Raised high, the gaps close and unlikely words get their chance. It is a nerve dial, not a knowledge dial.

How it reads the whole line

Before any bet is placed, every word in the line looks at every other word — the mechanism called attention. Each word builds its meaning from the company it keeps: in the line above, watched borrows from lighthouse and lamp, which is why the machine bets on sea rather than any noun the grammar would allow. The bet is one word; the reading behind it is the entire line at once.

Does it think ahead?

"It only predicts the next word" describes this page's interface, not necessarily the computation behind it. Interpretability research — opening models up and tracing the features inside — has caught larger models settling on a rhyme word before writing the line that reaches it, and holding one shared notion of a concept across languages. No plan is ever stated; something plan-shaped can still exist in the machinery. The honest phrase is not "no plan" but "no plan it ever declares."

Why the guesses seem rough

This press is deliberately tiny — a 135-million-parameter model, small enough to run inside a browser tab. The great models are thousands of times larger and far more fluent. What you see here is not their eloquence but their method, which is identical: odds on the next token, then a roll of the dice.

The honest caveat

Real systems add further rules after temperature — trimming the longest shots (top-p, top-k) and steering by training. Those are omitted here so a single idea stands clear. The sampling below draws from the full temperature-scaled odds, nothing more.
